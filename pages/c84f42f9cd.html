Andrej Karpathy shares his personal note-taking approach: the 'append-and-review' note. He's been seeding his Bear blog with more posts, and this one describes a method that's worked well for him over many years, striking a balance between being thorough and being practical.

The approach is simple: maintain a running note where you append thoughts, ideas, and observations as they come up throughout the day. Periodically review and organize. The key insight is that the append step should be frictionless (just add to the end) while the review step is where structure and connections emerge. This prevents the common failure mode of elaborate note-taking systems: spending more time organizing notes than actually thinking.

Karpathy's endorsement carries weight because he's known for systematic thinking and deep technical work. His note-taking method reflects the same philosophy he brings to AI research: start with raw data (observations), let patterns emerge through review, and resist premature organization. It's a practical counterpoint to the elaborate second-brain and Zettelkasten approaches that many knowledge workers get lost in.
