Zeno Rocha, founder of Resend, codifies how startups maintain momentum into a framework he calls 'The Heartbeat.' The core premise is deceptively simple but hard to execute: getting momentum is extremely hard, losing it is very easy. As makers, you not only have to ship software ‚Äî you have to ship AND talk about it. Constantly. If you want to create a feeling of 'always shipping,' you need a rhythm.

The framework works like an actual heartbeat ‚Äî big peaks followed by small peaks, with the beat always present. A flat line means you're dead. No momentum. The balance between big and small peaks matters, but the beat must never stop.

The six layers, from highest impact to highest frequency:

A) Launch Weeks (2-3x/year) ‚Äî The big peaks. 5 new features in 5 days. Most impactful but highest cost to the team. Don't do them too frequently.

B) Mini-Launches (4-5x/year) ‚Äî Small wins worth sharing in detail, without the full coordination overhead of a Launch Week.

C) Changelogs (1-2x/month) ‚Äî Regular product updates showing evolution. Pro tip: create an internal streak to motivate the team so you never miss a cycle.

D) Blog posts (1-2x/month) ‚Äî Nothing is more telling than a company blog with the last post from a year ago. It signals death.

E) Customer stories (1-2x/month) ‚Äî Real developer experiences. Crucial for attracting new customers AND keeping the internal team motivated with proof that the product matters.

F) Social posts (1x/day) ‚Äî Be very active but in only a few places. For Resend, X and LinkedIn worked best.

Each layer has real-life examples from Resend's own execution. The framework has been widely adopted by developer-focused startups as a practical playbook for developer marketing.
