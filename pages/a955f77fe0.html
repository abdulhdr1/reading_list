The Wikipedia article on Parkinson's Law of Triviality ‚Äî the observation that organizations give disproportionate weight to trivial issues. The classic example: a committee approving plans for a nuclear power plant spends most of its time debating what materials to use for the bicycle shed, because everyone can understand a bike shed but few can evaluate reactor design.

The concept, coined by C. Northcote Parkinson in 1957, was popularized in software development by FreeBSD developer Poul-Henning Kamp in 1999, leading to the terms 'bikeshedding' and 'bike-shed effect.' The principle states: 'The time spent on any item of the agenda will be in inverse proportion to the sum of money involved.'

Bikeshedding is ubiquitous in software development: teams spend hours debating variable naming conventions while glossing over architectural decisions that will shape the codebase for years. The concept is essential for any leader or contributor to understand ‚Äî recognizing bikeshedding when it happens is the first step to redirecting energy toward decisions that actually matter.
