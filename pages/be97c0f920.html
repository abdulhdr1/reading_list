Dan Luu debunks Jeff Atwood's influential post arguing against ECC (Error-Correcting Code) memory. Atwood's arguments: Google didn't use ECC in 1999, most errors are hard errors, hardware has improved, and if ECC mattered it'd be used everywhere. Luu dismantles each point.

Google abandoned non-ECC servers shortly after deploying them, recognizing it was a serious error. Cargo-culting Google's experiments is particularly dangerous because Google's 'moonshot' culture meant many experiments failed ‚Äî including putting servers in shipping containers (deemed unsuccessful) and designs that caused actual fires. More importantly, Google's own research (published after Atwood's post) showed RAM error rates were far higher than commonly believed.

The deeper lesson: authoritative-sounding blog posts from popular writers can spread misinformation that persists for years. Atwood's post convinced many people that ECC was unnecessary, but the underlying reasoning was flawed ‚Äî it relied on outdated information, survivorship bias, and the assumption that market prevalence equals correctness. Luu's piece is a masterclass in questioning received wisdom, even from respected sources.
