Dan Luu analyzes Travis CI data across popular GitHub projects and finds that open source builds are broken far more often than corporate ones. At work, 99% uptime on the build would be considered bad (3.5 days of failures per year). But only 8 of 40 popular GitHub projects achieve even that level, and 15 of 40 don't achieve 90% uptime.

The diagnosis: at work, broken builds cost visible money (N idle engineers √ó X hours = $Y wasted). Open source projects lack this accountability feedback loop. Contributors' time is wasted invisibly. Web developers obsess over 100ms of page load latency affecting conversion rates ‚Äî but what's the 'conversion rate' effect when a potential contributor spends 20 minutes setting up dependencies only to find the build is broken?

The article highlights a systemic problem in open source: the metrics that matter (contributor experience, onboarding friction, build reliability) are invisible by default, while the metrics that get attention (stars, downloads, features) are highly visible. It's a classic case of what gets measured gets managed ‚Äî and what doesn't get measured gets neglected.
